RAINBOW
Rainbows, those ephemeral spectacles of natural beauty, emerge from the interplay between sunlight and moisture in the atmosphere, embodying a phenomenon where science and wonder intertwine. As beams of sunlight encounter water droplets suspended in the air, a series of optical processes unfold. Initially, the light refracts as it enters each droplet, bending and splitting into its constituent colors. This dispersion of light continues inside the droplet, with each color bending at slightly different angles due to their varying wavelengths, creating a spectrum.
This spectrum of colors is then reflected off the inner surface of the water droplet and refracts once more as it exits, reaching the observer's eye as a radiant arc across the sky. From red at its outer edge to violet at its innermost, a rainbow displays the full breadth of visible light, offering a splendid visual explanation of the dispersion and refraction of sunlight. Beyond their breathtaking beauty, rainbows serve as a portal to the study of the sun and atmospheric sciences, allowing scientists to explore the properties of light and the complex ways in which it interacts with the elements of our world.
